# Premium Enterprise Sales Playbook

## Purpose
This playbook gives Monyawn a controlled, repeatable premium enterprise sales motion that stays aligned to current product truth.

It is intended for:
- CEO
- President / GM or CRO-equivalent
- Deal Desk / Commercial Operations Lead
- Enterprise Counsel / Contracting Lead
- Solutions Demo Lead
- Diligence Packet / Sales Enablement Lead

## Product Truth First
The playbook assumes the current product posture is:
- local-first
- export-first
- human-reviewable
- multi-opportunity by default
- not a hosted sovereign deployment offer today

If a deal requires claims outside that boundary, route it for controlled review instead of stretching the story.

## Who Owns What

| Role | Primary responsibility |
|---|---|
| CEO | Category framing, executive sponsorship, strategic account judgment |
| President / GM or CRO-equivalent | Pipeline quality, deal progression, commercial accountability |
| Deal Desk / Commercial Operations Lead | Packaging, approvals, commercial discipline |
| Enterprise Counsel / Contracting Lead | Contract posture, redlines, non-standard commitment review |
| Solutions Demo Lead | Demo narrative, product proof, buyer-context adaptation |
| Diligence Packet / Sales Enablement Lead | Packet assembly, buyer follow-up materials, source-of-truth routing |
| Customer Assurance / Security Questionnaire Lead | Security and diligence answer quality |

## Ideal Premium Motion
1. Qualify the buyer against pursuit criteria.
2. Send the one-pager or buyer packet first.
3. Run the executive narrative and local-first posture clearly.
4. Demo the product with current-state truth.
5. Route diligence through the controlled packet and answer bank.
6. Move pricing, packaging, and contract exceptions through the approval ladder.
7. Close only what the product and trust posture can actually support.

## Qualification Questions
- Is the buyer comfortable with a current local-first posture?
- Are they evaluating workflow governance and human-reviewable AI rather than demanding unsupported hosted claims?
- Is there a real operator or sponsor who understands why local control matters?
- Is the use case close enough to the current product shape to avoid custom-product drift?
- Can the buyer follow the controlled diligence path instead of demanding ad hoc commitments?

## Demo Structure
Use this sequence:
1. Product posture and data boundary
2. Multi-opportunity workspace
3. Candidate story and guided workflow
4. Staff and admin controls
5. Export package and buyer packet generation
6. Trust-center and diligence routing

## Objection Guidance

| Objection | Response posture |
|---|---|
| “Do you host this for us?” | Explain current local-first posture first, then route hosted/deployment requests through deployment review materials. |
| “Can you guarantee sovereignty?” | Do not imply support; route to sovereign posture and deployment review documents. |
| “Can you sign our standard security language?” | Use the questionnaire kit and redline guidance before answering. |
| “Can users lose data?” | Explain local storage limits and export-first recovery clearly. |
| “Why should we trust this if it is local-first?” | Emphasize user custody, reviewability, and explicit governance surfaces. |

## Exit Criteria For A Healthy Opportunity
- sponsor understands current product posture
- demo matched documented truth
- diligence packet path was accepted
- commercial packaging stayed inside approval rules
- contract language did not outrun deployment and trust posture

## Stop Conditions
Slow or pause the pursuit when:
- the buyer requires unsupported hosted or sovereign commitments
- legal terms imply controls the company has not approved
- the team is improvising answers instead of using source documents
- the prospect is treating roadmap items as current offers

## Evidence Of Good Execution
- buyer packet sent in the right order
- demo stays within product truth
- diligence questions answered from controlled materials
- pricing and legal exceptions are visibly approved
- no unsupported claims appear in slides, emails, or calls
